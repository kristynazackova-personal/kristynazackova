Life coaching has a branding problem. Half of what's marketed under that label is genuinely transformative, and the other half is a stranger on Instagram telling you to manifest. So when I tell people I hired a coach, I get a fairly predictable split: the people who've worked with one nod, and the people who haven't raise an eyebrow.

I'm in the eyebrow-raising camp by temperament. I'm an analytical person, I read too much, and my default move when stuck is *more frameworks*. I hired a coach anyway. This is a short field report on that decision — what pushed me to do it, what I expected, what I actually got, and the part of the experience nobody seems to talk about: **the work between sessions, which is where most of the change actually happens.**

If you're a builder, operator, or PM type who suspects coaching might be useful but feels slightly silly about it — this is for you.

---

I hired a coach about a year into a stretch where, on paper, things were going well.

New role. New product. New context. The kind of period where, if someone had pulled my LinkedIn, they would have seen progress. What they wouldn't have seen was that I'd been quietly ignoring the same three or four questions for months — about scope, energy, what I actually wanted next — and every time I sat down to think about them, I produced a beautifully reasoned plan I then didn't follow.

That last part was the giveaway. Not the unanswered questions. The pattern of *answering them and then not acting on the answers.*

## What I Expected

If I'm honest, I expected one of two things:

1. **A productivity upgrade.** Better goals. A cleaner system. A version of me that ran my week the way I ran my projects.
2. **A polite waste of money.** Someone asking me "how does that make you feel?" while I waited for the hour to end.

I got neither. The thing I actually got was harder to describe and more useful than either.

## What I Actually Got

Three things, in roughly the order of how surprising they were.

### 1. Someone whose job was to not let me get away with it

I talk to a lot of smart people. Friends, colleagues, mentors. They're generous with their time and they care about me. But they also like me, and they have to keep liking me after the conversation, which means there's a soft ceiling on how directly they can push.

A good coach doesn't have that ceiling. Their job is *exactly* the part that the rest of my support network can't quite do: notice the inconsistency, name the avoidance, ask the question I was hoping nobody would ask. The first session where she said something like *"You've described this exact problem in three different ways in the last twenty minutes — what are you not letting yourself say?"* — that was the moment the money started feeling well-spent.

### 2. A reliable container for thinking

I'm a thinker by trade. I do not, as it turns out, reliably make time to think *about my own life* with the same rigor I bring to a roadmap. There's always something more urgent.

A recurring 60-minute slot, on the calendar, with a person who expects me to show up with something — turns out that's enough infrastructure to do the thinking I claimed I wanted to do. The session itself is barely the point; the *forcing function* is. I'd argue at least half the value of coaching is just that: a non-negotiable appointment with your own life.

### 3. The work between sessions

This is the part nobody warned me about and the part that changed the most.

The sessions are an hour. The week between them is the other 167 hours. Most of the actual change happened *in* those 167 hours: noticing in real time, in a meeting, that I was about to do the exact thing we'd named the previous week. Catching myself mid-spiral and using a tool we'd practiced. Making a small, in-the-moment choice differently because we'd already rehearsed the harder version of it.

Good coaching is not a series of insights you have on a Zoom call. Good coaching is a slow rewiring of your defaults, with a structured weekly checkpoint to compare notes.

## What It Wasn't

A few things people often think coaching is, that it isn't, at least not for me.

- **Not therapy.** Coaching looks forward. It works on patterns and goals and choices. It doesn't pretend to handle trauma, clinical issues, or the deeper structural stuff — and a good coach will tell you when you've crossed into territory that needs a different professional. Mine did.
- **Not advice.** I expected to be told what to do. I almost never was. The work is mostly questions, and the questions are sharper than mine.
- **Not a hype person.** If you want someone to tell you you're amazing, hire a friend group, not a coach. A coach who only encourages you isn't doing the job.

## The Things I Wish I'd Done Differently

- **I would have hired one earlier.** I waited until I was visibly stuck. The biggest unlock from coaching isn't getting unstuck — it's avoiding the next stuckness, six months out, that you can't yet see.
- **I would have been pickier on fit.** I got lucky with my first one, but I've since talked to enough people to know fit varies wildly. Most coaches will give you a free intro call. Take the call. Talk to three. Notice who you're slightly afraid to be honest with — that's usually the right one.
- **I would have committed to a real chunk of time.** Three sessions is a sample. Twelve is a project. The work between sessions is what compounds, and compounding takes runway.

## What I'd Tell Someone Considering It

A short version, since this is the question I get most often.

1. **You don't need to be in crisis.** Most of my clients now aren't. They're high-functioning people in a fuzzy phase: a transition, a promotion, a question that won't sit down. Coaching works *especially* well there, because the stakes are real but the structure is loose.
2. **You do need to be willing to be uncomfortable.** If you want to be told you're already doing it right, save your money. The whole product is a person who's allowed to push.
3. **Pick the medium that fits your life.** I do video. Some people prefer phone. The format matters less than consistency.
4. **Treat it like training, not therapy.** Show up with something. Take notes. Try the thing between sessions. The clients who get the most out of coaching are the ones who run the week as the actual work and the call as the review.

## Final Take

The ROI on a good coach, for me, has not looked like productivity gains, even though those came along for the ride. It's looked like *fewer months lost to the same loop.* The patterns I would have spent another half a year not naming, named in a session. The decisions I would have postponed, made.

That's the part that's hard to put a price on, and the part I underestimated as a skeptic: not what I learned, but **how much faster I stopped repeating myself.**

If you've been circling the idea of hiring a coach, the most useful thing I can tell you is the one thing I most resisted hearing: *the call is the small part of the work.* If you're willing to put in the 167 hours, the hour itself is one of the highest-leverage things you can buy.
